**Opinion & analysis** · 8 min read

![Adam Mosseri on the TED 2022 stage](/images/adam.png)

There's a moment in Adam Mosseri's TED 2022 talk that sticks with you. The man
who runs Instagram — the platform with more than two billion users, the heart of
the modern influencer economy — walks on stage and says, with a calm that throws
you off, that the power of platforms is going to end. And that this is a good
thing.

He doesn't say it as a threat or an apology. He says it as a diagnosis. And he
adds something no social-media executive had said in public before:

> "No company can take Lisa's community away from her. Instagram could disappear
> tomorrow, and she would keep her relationship with her subscribers and keep her
> income."
>
> — Adam Mosseri, Head of Instagram · TED 2022

And he follows it up with brutal honesty:

> "Meta can't build this. No single company can."
>
> — Adam Mosseri

It's worth pausing on that. The man who runs the largest platform for creators in
the world is saying, on the most-watched stage on the planet, that the current
model is unsustainable — and that the solution can't come from a centralized
company.

That was three years ago. And he was right.

## What Mosseri saw coming

The talk is called *A Creator-Led Internet, Built on Blockchain*. Mosseri
describes a future where blockchain removes the need for the intermediary — the
platform — between a creator and their audience. Where creators own their
relationship with their fans, their income, their digital identity.

He calls it "the greatest transfer of power from institutions to individuals of
all time." It's not a technical talk about NFTs or cryptocurrencies. It's a
philosophical argument about who should control the relationship between a person
and their community.

The answer Mosseri gives is clear: not the platforms. History, he says, has
always taken power from institutions and handed it to people. The printing press
took it from the church. The internet took it from the media. The blockchain is
going to take it from social networks.

Three years after that talk, the question is no longer whether it will happen.
The question is how — and who is actually building it.

## The problem Mosseri didn't solve

There's an irony in the talk worth naming. Mosseri describes the problem with
crystal clarity, but he doesn't have the solution. And he admits it himself: Meta
can't build it. A centralized company can't create a truly decentralized system
without destroying its own business model.

The attempts at social tokenization over the past few years always hit the same
wall: technical complexity. To buy your favorite creator's token you needed to
install a wallet, understand what a seed phrase is, buy crypto on an exchange, pay
fees in a currency you didn't understand. They were products for the 2% of the
population that already lived in crypto.

The other 98% — the real fans, the lifelong followers, the people who pay for
YouTube memberships and Super Thanks on livestreams — were left out. Not for lack
of interest. For friction.

## What Matiz solves

Matiz Protocol starts exactly from Mosseri's diagnosis and solves the friction
problem he left unanswered.

A fan can buy their favorite creator's token with their credit card, from their
phone, in under a minute. No wallets. No seed phrases. No prior crypto. The
blockchain is there, doing the work under the hood, invisible and reliable —
exactly as it should be.

And the part Mosseri described as "what no company can build": the USDC reserve
backing every token is locked in a smart contract that nobody can touch. Not the
creator, not the platform, not us. It's not a promise. It's code. Anyone can
verify it.

> "Your audience on Instagram is borrowed. Your economy on Matiz is yours."
>
> — Matiz Protocol

## The question Mosseri left open — and Matiz answers

There's a moment in the talk where Mosseri poses the central question: how can a
creator prove the real value of their audience?

Today, in 2025, that question is more urgent than it was in 2022. Artificial
intelligence makes it trivial to fabricate followers, comments, likes, even
entire conversations. Brands know it. The marketing teams that used to pay
$50,000 for an influencer post are asking for proof that traditional metrics can
no longer provide.

A token holder is the one metric that can't be faked. To hold a creator's token,
someone put up real money. They didn't click a button. They didn't follow in
exchange for a giveaway. They made an economic decision. That is verifiable
on-chain, in real time, by anyone in the world.

That's what Matiz gives a creator today: the ability to tell a brand — "I have
2,000 people who bet real money on me. Here's the link to the blockchain. Go
verify it."

## Why does it matter that Mosseri said it?

It might seem odd to use the Head of Instagram's talk to discuss a platform that,
in a sense, competes with Instagram. But that's not how we see it.

Mosseri wasn't promoting a competitor. He was describing a reality he saw coming
and that, honestly, he admitted he couldn't stop. Centralized platforms will keep
existing. But the creator who understands what's happening will also want their
own economy — portable, inviolable.

Instagram for visibility. Matiz for the economy. They're not mutually exclusive.
They're complementary.

Mosseri's talk has nearly two million views on TED.com. It's one of the
most-watched of TED 2022. That says something about how many people already feel
the current model isn't enough — and are looking for what comes next.

If you're a creator, the moment to understand this is before it's obvious. The

If you're a fan, the moment to support your favorite creator in a way that has

And if you're a brand looking for verifiable audiences in a world where followers
